## 1. One-Sentence Game Pitch

**Trust Me Bro** is a 2–4 player online co-op action roguelite where each player controls their own hero, but all heroes are physically bound into one shared body — forcing the team to survive together until the final stage turns into direct individual combat.

---

## 2. Short Positioning Summary

Trust Me Bro is not a standard co-op game.

It is a **social chaos action game** built around:
- forced cooperation
- shared mistakes
- tension between teamwork and selfishness
- run-based progression
- dilemma-driven choices
- a final betrayal phase where allies become opponents

The emotional promise is not simply “beat monsters together.”

The emotional promise is:

- laugh together
- blame each other
- suspect each other
- survive by cooperating
- then face each other in a final showdown

This game should feel like:
**“We barely survived together… and now we have to fight each other.”**

## 4. What Makes Trust Me Bro Different

### 4.1 Linked-Body Co-op
Each player controls **their own character**, but those characters are **bound to one shared body**.

This means:
- players are not independently roaming around the map
- movement and combat responsibility are distributed across the group
- one player’s mistake affects everyone
- cooperation is not optional; it is built into the body itself

This is one of the game’s strongest unique hooks and should be explained clearly.

### 4.2 Shared Struggle, Separate Responsibility
Even though the group is physically linked, each player still feels ownership over:
- their role
- their timing
- their decisions
- their contribution
- their failures

This creates a powerful social effect:
players can always argue about **who caused the disaster**.

### 4.3 Co-op First, Final Betrayal Later
Most of the run is about surviving together.

But the final stage changes the emotional contract:
- the team reaches the final room through cooperation
- then the players are forced into direct individual combat
- the people who carried you, blocked you, saved you, or sabotaged you become your final opponents

This final reversal is one of the game’s defining features and should be present in major marketing copy.

### 4.4 Choice-Driven Tension
Runs are shaped by risky choices:
- room choices
- reward decisions
- dilemma events
- selfish versus group-benefiting outcomes
- decisions that create tension long before the final showdown

This means the run is not only about action skill.
It is also about group psychology.

## 5. Core Gameplay Explanation

### Basic Loop
A run generally works like this:
1. enter a run with 2–4 players
2. move through a sequence of rooms / encounters
3. fight enemies and survive co-op combat
4. choose paths, rewards, and dilemma outcomes
5. grow stronger, but also create more tension inside the team
6. reach the final stage
7. shift from cooperation into direct individual confrontation

### Room / Encounter Texture
Possible room types include:
- normal combat rooms
- elite encounters
- dilemma rooms
- recovery rooms
- accident / event rooms
- final room / final stage

### Progression Feel
The game should feel like a run steadily becoming:
- stronger
- messier
- more suspicious
- more emotionally unstable

The point is not clean mastery.
The point is escalating social pressure.

---

## 6. The Final Stage Matters

This is critical.

Do **not** describe Trust Me Bro like it is only a co-op game.

The final stage is where the structure pays off:
- players survive together first
- then enter individual combat
- earlier choices, upgrades, resentment, greed, and distrust now matter in a new way
- teamwork was necessary to get here
- betrayal, rivalry, and self-preservation decide who wins

A strong phrasing pattern:
- **Cooperate to survive. Compete to finish.**
- **Work together until you can’t trust each other anymore.**
- **Co-op gets you there. Betrayal decides the winner.**

### Long Description
Trust Me Bro is built around one simple idea: teamwork is funniest when it starts falling apart.

Each player controls their own hero, but the group is physically linked into one shared body. That means every run is built on cooperation, shared mistakes, and constant blame. Fight through dangerous rooms, defeat enemies, collect upgrades, and make high-tension decisions that shape both your strength and your relationships.

But this is not just a co-op run. The final stage turns allies into rivals, forcing players into direct individual combat after surviving the journey together. Everything the team argued about along the way — upgrades, choices, greed, timing, mistakes — suddenly matters even more.

Trust Me Bro is a game about surviving together just long enough to stop trusting each other.

## 18. FAQ Reference Answers

### What kind of game is Trust Me Bro?
Trust Me Bro is a 2–4 player online co-op action roguelite built around linked-body teamwork, risky choices, and a final stage where players turn against each other.

### Is it a co-op game or a competitive game?
It starts as a co-op survival experience and ends in direct individual combat. Cooperation gets you to the final stage. Rivalry decides who wins.

### How many players does it support?
Trust Me Bro is designed for 2–4 players online.

### What makes it different?
Each player controls their own hero, but everyone is physically bound into one shared body. This creates forced cooperation, shared mistakes, and a very different kind of multiplayer tension.

### Is it meant to be funny or serious?
The game is designed to be tense, chaotic, and funny. The main emotional goal is to create strong reactions, memorable failures, and “who caused that?” moments.

## 22. Final Writing Rules for Cursor

When writing copy for the Trust Me Bro website:

1. Lead with the emotional hook first.
2. Explain the linked-body concept clearly and early.
3. Make it obvious that this is 2–4 player online multiplayer.
4. Do not forget the final-stage shift into direct individual combat.
5. Translate mechanics into social outcomes.
6. Prefer memorable, high-contrast wording over generic marketing filler.
7. Keep the tone playful, sharp, and confident.
8. Make people imagine playing it with friends.
9. Avoid making the game sound like a generic party game or a generic PvP game.
10. Always preserve the game’s strongest promise:

**You survive together first. Then you stop trusting each other.**
